› can these results be proven analytically


• Some can, some probably need computational certificates.

  Likely Analytically Provable

  - m1 = 2 universal bound: yes. The polynomial identity
    -c3((c1-c2)^2+c1+c2) is already an analytic proof.

  - T(7,1,9) permanent crossover: yes. The quotient has a small spectral form; once the sign flips and the subdominant ratio decays monotonically, permanence is analytic.
  - Double-cover lemma: yes. Pure homomorphism lifting argument.
  - B(x,y) spectral family: yes. The 2x2 quotient gives exact eigenvalues and likely an analytic infinite-family theorem.
  - H18 walk-table/margin: the existence at n=17 is exact finite arithmetic, but can be written as an analytic calculation from the quotient matrix.

  Maybe Analytically Provable, But Needs Work

  - Strongness of T^(1,28,1,36): probably. Need a spectral decomposition / recurrence proof showing the leading coefficient for E_n is smaller than for P_n, then separately handle pre-asymptotic sign.
  - H* as 15-vertex Leontovich: existence is finite exact arithmetic. Minimality below 15 likely remains computational unless you derive structural inequalities for all smaller bipartite pattern vectors.
  - The n=17340 even crossover: existence/threshold can be exact recurrence arithmetic; “no earlier even crossover” is computational unless you prove monotonicity over the recurrence tail and check a finite prefix.

  Hard / Probably Computational

  - “No graph on m <= 11 is Leontovich”: very hard analytically because it quantifies over all target graphs and all source trees. Current near-path filter is not enough.
  - “No smaller bipartite depth-2 graph exists”: likely computational enumeration unless reduced to a tractable finite symbolic inequality.
  - 76-vertex leaf-pruning minimality: computational certificate / exhaustive family proof, not naturally analytic.
  - Perturbed non-bipartite construction: could become analytic if you explicitly derive the quotient matrix after perturbation and prove its leading coefficient ratio is < 1.

  Best paper strategy: present finite exact computations as certified arithmetic lemmas, not pretend everything is hand-analytic. Then reserve “theorem” for claims whose quantifiers are fully covered by proof or exhaustive certificate.
