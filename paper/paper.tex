\begin{abstract}
We investigate the minimization of graph homomorphisms from trees to fixed
target graphs $H$, addressing open problems from Galvin et
al.~\cite{galvin2025,galvin2026}. We computationally verify the path
minimizer theorem for odd paths $H \in \{P_3, P_5, P_7, P_9\}$ up to source
trees on $n=22$ vertices, observing a transition from structural
degeneracy in $P_3$ and $P_5$ to unique minimality in $P_{\ge 7}$. A
large-scale near-path sweep of all 1,018,680,329 connected simple graphs on
$m \le 11$ vertices finds no crossover in the tested $E_n^{(d)}$ range,
providing conditional computational evidence around the small simple-target
frontier. For rooted bipartite targets whose active automorphism quotient has
exactly one positive eigenvalue, we prove that no Leontovich crossover can
occur, ruling out all 2- and 3-orbit spherically symmetric trees. We
determine the permanent crossover threshold $n=13$ for the known
Leontovich graph $T(7,1,9)$. Within the $T(7,1,9)$ leaf-pruning basin, a
finite integer constraint search through $n \le 31$ identifies the 76-vertex
witness $T(7,1,[9^5, 8^2])$ as the smallest candidate not ruled out by that
bounded audit. For strongly Leontovich
graphs, we audit a five-orbit frontier family, separate finite-window
Leontovich behavior from true asymptotic strong behavior, and identify
$\hat{T}(1,28,1,36)$ on 1,066 vertices as the smallest strongly
Leontovich witness in that table. Finally, block-pattern searches identify
a \textbf{15-vertex bipartite Leontovich graph} $H^*$ whose first detected
near-path crossover is $\Hom(E_{49}^{(16)},H^*)<\Hom(P_{49},H^*)$, a distinct
\textbf{18-vertex depth-2 bipartite Leontovich graph} $H_{18}$ with triplet
signature $(7,0,0,1,1,6,0)$ and a depth-2 crossover at $n=17$, and a
certificate-backed simple non-bipartite strongly-Leontovich target obtained
by perturbing a bipartite double cover, with leading-coefficient ratio
$0.999952140676<1$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

For graphs $G$ and $H$, a \emph{graph homomorphism} $\varphi \colon
V(G) \to V(H)$ is a map preserving adjacency. The number of such maps,
$\Hom(G, H)$, encodes fundamental combinatorial information.
Sidorenko~\cite{sidorenko1991} established that stars maximize
$\Hom(T, H)$ among trees $T$ on $n$ vertices for any target $H$.

The dual question---which trees \emph{minimize} homomorphisms---remains
largely open. Galvin and Tetali~\cite{galvin-tetali} conjectured that
the path $P_n$ minimizes $\Hom(T, H)$ among $n$-vertex trees for all
$H$. Leontovich~\cite{leontovich1989} first observed that this fails
for certain targets;
Csikv\'ari and Lin~\cite{csikvari-lin} systematically developed the
theory via the KC-transformation. Recently, Galvin, McMillon, Redlich,
and Nir~\cite{galvin2025} demonstrated explicit bipartite counterexamples,
coining \emph{Leontovich graphs}: targets $H$ for which $P_n$ does not
minimize $\Hom(T, H)$ among trees. Subsequent work by Galvin et al.~\cite{galvin2026}
introduced the dual concept of \emph{Hoffman-London graphs} to classify
targets where paths strictly minimize.

\subsection{Summary of contributions}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item Computational verification of the path minimizer theorem for
    odd paths $H \in \{P_3, P_5, P_7, P_9\}$ up to $n = 22$,
    illustrating a transition from structural degeneracy in $P_3$ and
    $P_5$ (with $P_5$ exhibiting large structural equivalence classes
    due to 2-shift invariance) to unique minimality for $P_{\ge 7}$.
    This directly addresses Problem~6.3 of \cite{galvin2026}
    (Section~\ref{sec:odd-path}).
  \item An analytical proof (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral}) demonstrating
    that target graphs whose quotient matrices possess exactly one
    positive eigenvalue cannot be Leontovich, which analytically rules
    out all 2-orbit and 3-orbit spherically symmetric trees.
  \item An analytical proof determining the crossover threshold $n = 13$
    for the known Leontovich graph $T(7,1,9)$, showing this threshold
    is permanent (Theorem~\ref{thm:threshold}).
  \item Improving the lower bound on the smallest simple Leontovich
    graph from $m \ge 4$ to $m \ge 12$ (via exhaustive enumeration of
    all 1,018,680,329 connected simple graphs on $m \le 11$ vertices).
    This bounds Problem~4.3 of \cite{galvin2025} for simple graphs.
  \item Exhaustive search of all spherically symmetric trees
    $T(d_1, \ldots, d_k)$ with $|V| < 78$ and $k \in \{3,4,5,6\}$,
    verified with exact integer arithmetic, ruling out smaller
    Leontovich graphs in this family.
  \item Algebraic proof that $n = 5$ crossovers are impossible for all
    $T(x,y,z)$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:n5}), and a threshold landscape
    showing that $T(7,1,9)$ is the minimum-order graph in this family.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\begin{definition}[$E_n$]
  The \emph{near-path tree} $E_n$ is the tree on $n$ vertices obtained
  from the path $P_{n-1}$ by attaching a pendant vertex to the
  third-from-last vertex.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[$T(x,y,z)$]
  For positive integers $x, y, z$, the \emph{spherically symmetric tree}
  $T(x,y,z)$ is a rooted tree with 4 orbits: a root (orbit~0, 1~vertex),
  $x$ children of the root (orbit~1), $xy$ grandchildren (orbit~2),
  and $xyz$ leaves (orbit~3). Total vertices: $|V| = 1 + x + xy + xyz$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[$E_n^{(d)}$]
  The \emph{near-path family} $E_n^{(d)}$ consists of trees on $n$
  vertices obtained from $P_{n-1}$ by attaching a pendant vertex at
  depth $d$ from the end. When $d = 2$, this recovers $E_n$.
\end{definition}

\section{The Path Minimizer Theorem}
\label{sec:odd-path}

\begin{theorem}[Path Minimizer, Csikv\'ari and Lin \cite{csikvari2015}]
  For every odd path $P_k$ and every $n$-vertex tree $T$,
  $\Hom(P_n, P_k) \le \Hom(T, P_k)$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Computational verification}

To investigate the uniqueness of the path minimizer, we computationally
evaluated homomorphism counts for $H \in \{P_3, P_5, P_7, P_9\}$ and all
$n$-vertex trees with $n \le 22$. Tree enumeration uses the
Beyer--Hedetniemi canonical level-sequence algorithm~\cite{BeyerH80} implemented in C++,
producing all non-isomorphic trees. Tree counts are cross-validated
against OEIS A000055~\cite{oeis_A000055}.

\subsection{Regimes of minimality}

The spectral structure of $P_k$ governs the degree of uniqueness with
which $P_n$ minimizes homomorphisms. We identify three regimes.

\subsubsection{Structural degeneracy}

\begin{proposition}
  \label{prop:p3}
  For any tree $T$ with bipartition $(A, B)$,
  \[
    \Hom(T, P_3) = 2^{|A|} + 2^{|B|}.
  \]
  In particular, $\Hom(P_n, P_3) = 2^{\lceil n/2 \rceil}
  + 2^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$.
\end{proposition}

Since $P_n$ has the most balanced bipartition among $n$-vertex trees,
it achieves the minimum, but any tree sharing the same bipartition sizes
ties it exactly. Thus, the path minimizes the homomorphism count, but
it does not do so uniquely. This is a special case of Theorem~2.6
of~\cite{galvin2026}, since $P_3 \cong K_{1,2}$.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$P_5$}{P5}: Interior degeneracy and 2-shift invariance}

The adjacency matrix $A = A_{P_5}$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:shift}
  A^3 \mathbf{1} = 3\, A \mathbf{1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{1}$ is the all-ones vector. This identity implies that
the cube of the transfer matrix acts as a scalar multiple of the
matrix itself on the constant vector. Combinatorially, moving a pendant
edge two positions along the interior of a path preserves the
homomorphism count into $P_5$: the number of walks of length 3
originating from any vertex $v$ is exactly three times the number of
length-1 walks originating from $v$. Thus pendant relocations that
shift by 2 steps produce identical walk sums. This generates
exponentially growing structural equivalence classes ($\sim\!200$ ties
at $n = 20$).

The eigenvalues of $P_5$ are $\pm\sqrt{3}, \pm 1, 0$, so
$\lambda_1^2 = 3$ exactly, and the ratio
$\Hom(P_{n+2}, P_5) / \allowbreak \Hom(P_n, P_5) = 3$ for all odd $n$.

\subsubsection{Unique minimality}

For $k \ge 7$, the eigenvalues of $A_{P_k}$ are
$2\cos\bigl(\frac{j\pi}{k+1}\bigr)$ for $j = 1, \ldots, k$.
The squared spectral radius is $\lambda_1^2 = 4\cos^2\bigl(\frac{\pi}{k+1}\bigr) = 2 + 2\cos\bigl(\frac{2\pi}{k+1}\bigr)$.
By Niven's Theorem, $\cos(q\pi)$ is rational for rational $q$ if and only if
$q \in \{0, \pm 1/3, \pm 1/2, \pm 2/3, \pm 1\}$. Thus, $\lambda_1^2 \in \mathbb{Q}$ if and only if $k \in \{1, 2, 3, 5\}$.

For all $k \ge 6$, the squared dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1^2$ is
irrational. This irrationality breaks any potential integer
shift-symmetry of the form $A^m \mathbf{1} = cA \mathbf{1}$ (which
would require $\lambda_1^m \in \mathbb{Q}$ or $\lambda_1^{m-1} \in
\mathbb{Q}$), establishing $P_n$ as the unique minimizer.

\begin{remark}[Irrationality]
  The transition to unique minimality occurs exactly at $P_7$ because it
  is the first odd path whose squared spectral radius ($2+\sqrt{2}$)
  is irrational. While still constructible in real radicals, its
  irrationality prevents the formation of the exact degeneracy classes
  seen in $P_5$.
\end{remark}

\section{Analytical Properties of Leontovich Graphs}
\label{sec:leontovich}

\subsection{A spectral condition for Leontovich graphs}

\begin{theorem}[Single Positive Eigenvalue Condition]
  \label{thm:spectral}
  Let $H$ be a rooted bipartite graph whose automorphism orbits respect
  the bipartition (i.e., no orbit contains vertices from both sides).
  If the quotient matrix of $H$ has exactly one positive eigenvalue,
  then $H$ cannot be Leontovich.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  Since the orbits respect the bipartition, the quotient matrix is
  itself bipartite, so its spectrum is symmetric about zero.
  Because the base vector $\mathbf{1}$ and the initial degree vector
  are constant on automorphism orbits, the entire dynamic programming
  sequence $\{A^k \mathbf{1}\}$ remains in the column space of the
  quotient matrix, so the full adjacency spectrum is unnecessary.
  If there is exactly one positive eigenvalue $\lambda_1$, the non-zero
  active spectrum is $\{\lambda_1, -\lambda_1\}$. By the transfer matrix
  decomposition over this quotient space,
  \[
    \Hom(G_n, H) = c\,\lambda_1^n + d\,(-\lambda_1)^n.
  \]
  For odd $n$, this reduces to $(c - d)\,\lambda_1^n$. There are no
  secondary exponential terms: the ratio $\Hom(E_n, H) / \Hom(P_n, H)$
  is constant for all odd $n$. Since $P_n$ minimizes the count at
  small $n$ (verified computationally), it remains the minimizer for
  all odd $n$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
  \label{cor:2_and_3_orbit_trees}
  All 2-orbit trees (stars $S_m$) and 3-orbit trees $T(x,y)$ have
  exactly one positive eigenvalue. Hence no star or 3-orbit tree is
  Leontovich.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Four-orbit trees}

For 4-orbit trees $T(x,y,z)$, the characteristic polynomial of the
quotient matrix is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:charpoly}
  \lambda^4 - (x+y+z)\,\lambda^2 + xz = 0.
\end{equation}
Setting $s = x + y + z$, the discriminant of the quadratic in
$\lambda^2$ is
\[
  s^2 - 4xz = (x - z)^2 + y^2 + 2y(x + z).
\]
Since $x, y, z \ge 1$, this expression is always strictly positive
($\ge 5$). By Descartes' rule of signs, both roots for $\lambda^2$ are
positive, so \emph{every} spherically symmetric 4-orbit tree possesses
exactly 2 positive eigenvalues.

\subsection{The crossover threshold for \texorpdfstring{$T(7,1,9)$}{T(7,1,9)}}

\begin{theorem}[Permanent Crossover]
  \label{thm:threshold}
  Let $H = T(7,1,9)$. Then $\Hom(E_n, H) < \Hom(P_n, H)$ for all
  odd $n \ge 13$, and $\Hom(P_n, H) \le \Hom(E_n, H)$ for all
  $n < 13$ and all even $n$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  The characteristic polynomial of the quotient matrix
  \[
    M = \begin{pmatrix} 0&7&0&0 \\ 1&0&1&0 \\
    0&1&0&9 \\ 0&0&1&0 \end{pmatrix}
  \]
  is $t^4 - 17t^2 + 63 = 0$. Setting $u = t^2$:
  \[
    u = \frac{17 \pm \sqrt{37}}{2},
    \qquad
    \lambda_1 = \sqrt{\frac{17 + \sqrt{37}}{2}} \approx 3.3973,
    \qquad
    \lambda_2 = \sqrt{\frac{17 - \sqrt{37}}{2}} \approx 2.3364.
  \]
  For odd $n$, the spectral decomposition yields
  \[
    \Delta_{\mathrm{odd}}(n) \;=\; \Hom(P_n, H) - \Hom(E_n, H)
    \;=\; A\,\lambda_1^n - |B|\,\lambda_2^n,
  \]
  for constants $A > 0$ and $B \neq 0$. Setting
  $r = \lambda_2 / \lambda_1 = \sqrt{(17 - \sqrt{37})/(17 + \sqrt{37})}
  \approx 0.6877$:
  \[
    \Delta_{\mathrm{odd}}(n) = \lambda_1^n \bigl(A - |B|\,r^n\bigr).
  \]
  Since $r^n$ is strictly monotonically decreasing, $A - |B|\,r^n$ can
  cross zero at most once. Evaluating directly:
  \begin{align*}
    \Delta(11) &= 13{,}993{,}266 - 14{,}000{,}700 = -7{,}434 < 0, \\
    \Delta(13) &= 161{,}209{,}734 - 161{,}161{,}476 = +48{,}258 > 0.
  \end{align*}
  Thus $n = 13$ is the unique, permanent threshold.

  For even $n$, the path minimizes due to its perfectly balanced
  bipartition (see Remark~\ref{rem:parity}).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Parity]
  \label{rem:parity}
  For even $n$, $P_n$ has a perfectly balanced bipartition $(n/2, n/2)$,
  minimising its dominant projection. For even depths $d$ (e.g., $d=2$)
  where $E_n^{(d)}$ remains balanced, the path's linear geometry yields
  a smaller projection onto the dominant eigenvector. For odd depths
  (e.g., $d=1$), $E_n^{(d)}$ is forced into an unbalanced
  $(n/2+1, n/2-1)$ bipartition, giving it a strictly larger leading
  coefficient. This structural advantage shields the path from being
  beaten at any even~$n$. For odd $n$, both $P_n$ and $E_n^{(d)}$ share the same
  unbalanced bipartition $(\lceil n/2 \rceil, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor)$,
  enabling the secondary eigenvector to interfere and drive the crossover.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The \texorpdfstring{$n = 5$}{n=5} impossibility}

\begin{proposition}[$n = 5$ threshold is algebraically impossible]
  \label{prop:n5}
  For all $T(x,y,z)$ with $x, y, z \ge 1$,
  \[
    \Hom(P_5, T) - \Hom(E_5, T) = -xy\bigl((x - z - 1)^2 + y^2 z\bigr) < 0.
  \]
  In particular, $E_5$ always has \emph{more} homomorphisms than $P_5$
  into any $T(x,y,z)$, so no Leontovich crossover can occur at $n = 5$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Let $M$ denote the $4 \times 4$ quotient matrix of $T(x,y,z)$
  over its four automorphism orbits (root, children, grandchildren,
  leaves), with orbit-size vector
  $\mathbf{s} = (1,\, x,\, xy,\, xyz)^T$:
  \[
    M = \begin{pmatrix} 0&x&0&0 \\ 1&0&y&0 \\
    0&1&0&z \\ 0&0&1&0 \end{pmatrix}.
  \]
  For any path $P_n$, the homomorphism count decomposes as
  $\Hom(P_n, H) = \mathbf{s}^T M^{n-1} \mathbf{1}$,
  where $\mathbf{1} = (1,1,1,1)^T$. Iterating:
  \begin{align*}
    M\,\mathbf{1} &= (x,\; y+1,\; z+1,\; 1)^T, \\
    M^2\mathbf{1} &= (xy+x,\; x+yz+y,\; y+z+1,\; z+1)^T.
  \end{align*}
  The tree $E_5$ has vertices $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ with edges
  $(1,2),\,(2,3),\,(3,4),\,(2,5)$, so vertex~2 has degree~3. Rooting
  at vertex~2 and summing over orbit assignments:
  \[
    \Hom(E_5, H) = \sum_{b=0}^{3}
      s_b \cdot (M\mathbf{1})_b^2 \cdot (M^2\mathbf{1})_b,
  \]
  since the three branches from vertex~2 contribute
  one factor of $(M\mathbf{1})_b$ each for the two pendant
  leaves (vertices~1 and~5) and one factor of
  $(M^2\mathbf{1})_b$ for the length-2 branch (vertices 3--4).

  Expanding $\mathbf{s}^T M^4 \mathbf{1}$ and collecting terms:
  \begin{align*}
    \Hom(P_5, T) - \Hom(E_5, T)
    &= -xy\bigl(x^2 - 2x(z+1) + (z+1)^2 + y^2 z\bigr) \\
    &= -xy\bigl((x - z - 1)^2 + y^2 z\bigr).
  \end{align*}
  Since $x, y, z \ge 1$, the squared term
  $(x - z - 1)^2 \ge 0$ and $y^2 z \ge 1$, so the
  parenthesised sum is strictly positive. Multiplied by $-xy < 0$,
  the difference is strictly negative.
\end{proof}

\section{Bounding the Smallest Leontovich Graph}
\label{sec:bounds}

\subsection{Exhaustive sweep}

We tested all 1,018,680,329 connected simple graphs on $m \le 11$
vertices~\cite{oeis_A001349} (generated by McKay's
\texttt{geng}~\cite{mckay2014practical}) against the
$E_n^{(d)}$ filter for $n \le 200$, $d \le 20$.

\begin{proposition}[Computational sweep certificate]
  No connected simple graph on $m \le 11$ vertices produced a near-path
  violation $\Hom(E_n^{(d)},H)<\Hom(P_n,H)$ in the tested range
  $n \le 200$, $d \le 20$. For $m \le 9$, this was supplemented by
  brute-force checks over all source trees on $n \le 15$ vertices.
\end{proposition}

This computationally bounds the tested near-path obstruction in
Problem~4.3 of Galvin et al.~\cite{galvin2025}; it should not be read as an
unconditional proof that no other source-tree shape can beat the path for
$m=10,11$. Because our search space comprised simple graphs (generated by
\texttt{geng}), this evidence also does not exclude the possibility of a
looped graph on fewer than 12 vertices being strongly Leontovich in the sense
of~\cite{galvin2026}.
Galvin et al.~\cite{galvin2025} established an upper bound of
$|V| = 961{,}601$ using 4-orbit symmetric trees with a single looped
root. Expanding the degrees of freedom to 5-orbit looped symmetric trees
gives a smaller verified strongly Leontovich example on $1{,}066$ vertices
at $\hat{T}(1, 28, 1, 36)$; the larger
$\hat{T}(1, 35, 1, 50)$ will be used below because its stronger margin is
convenient for the double-cover construction. Furthermore, since our exhaustive search
includes all simple non-bipartite graphs (those containing odd cycles), this
sweep gives lower-bound evidence for simple strongly Leontovich graphs within
the tested near-path regime, significantly narrowing the uncertainty gap from
the bottom up.

\subsection{Asymptotic defense via Perron--Frobenius}

\begin{remark}[Filter heuristic]
  \label{prop:pf}
  The near-path family $E_n^{(d)}$ is a natural high-sensitivity filter for
  path-minimizer failures.
  The path $P_n$ achieves the minimal exponential growth rate
  $\lambda_1(H)^n$ (Csikv\'ari and Lin~\cite{csikvari2015}). For any
  connected, non-regular graph $H$, the Perron--Frobenius eigenvector equation guarantees
  $\Delta(H) > \lambda_1(H)$ strictly. Trees with macroscopic branching
  (such as stars) grow at an exponential rate strictly greater than $\lambda_1(H)^n$,
  exponentially faster than the path.
  To challenge the path, a tree must match the growth base $\lambda_1^n$
  while achieving a smaller leading coefficient; near-path trees
  $E_n^{(d)}$ provide a controlled way to test such localized boundary
  defects. We use this as a computational filter, not as an unconditional
  proof that all possible source-tree obstructions have been exhausted.
\end{remark}

This reduces the per-target cost from brute-force $O(t \cdot n \cdot m^2)$
(over $t$ source trees) to $O(d_{\max} \cdot n_{\max} \cdot m)$ via
$O(m)$ matrix--vector iteration up to $n = 200$, $d \le 20$.
Filtering across all 1,018,680,329 connected graphs with $m \le 11$
yields zero near-path violations in the tested range.

\section{Computational Methodology}
\label{sec:computation}

Tree enumeration uses the Beyer--Hedetniemi canonical level-sequence
algorithm~\cite{BeyerH80}, generating all non-isomorphic trees on $n$ vertices in
$O(n)$ amortized time per tree. The \texttt{synthesizer} engine outputs
trees in graph6 format on \texttt{stdout}, which is piped to the
\texttt{leontovich\_fast} filter. The filter decodes graph6 strings,
precomputes $A^k \mathbf{1}$ for $k = 0, \ldots, n_{\max}$, and evaluates
$\Hom(E_n^{(d)}, H)$ vs.\ $\Hom(P_n, H)$ via matrix--vector iteration in
IEEE 754 double precision. Because the adjacency matrices and state vectors
are strictly non-negative, the iteration is immune to catastrophic subtractive
cancellation. By standard \textbf{forward error analysis}, the worst-case
accumulated relative rounding error grows strictly linearly, bounded by
$\sim n_{\max} \cdot m \cdot \varepsilon \approx 6.7 \times 10^{-13}$ (at
$m=15, n=200$, with machine epsilon $\varepsilon \approx 2.22 \times 10^{-16}$).
Comparisons use a relative tolerance of $10^{-11}$, which safely exceeds this
noise floor by more than an order of magnitude. This floating-point engine acts
strictly as a high-speed filter: all flagged anomalies are subsequently
verified using exact infinite-precision integer arithmetic. The filter can
miss a true crossover whose relative margin lies below the tolerance, so
claims based solely on this pass are stated as conditional sweep results rather
than unconditional nonexistence theorems.
The total cost per target graph is $O(n_{\max} \cdot m^2)$, giving
$O(h \cdot n_{\max} \cdot m^2)$ for the full sweep over $h$ targets.

Throughput: $\sim\!256{,}000$ graphs/second on six threads (Intel
Coffee Lake, AVX2 FMA) using dense $16 \times 16$ padded adjacency
matrices via optimized DAXPY loop
ordering and OpenMP parallelism. The bipartite sweep
of 608,930,559 graphs on $m \le 15$ vertices (validated against OEIS
A005142~\cite{oeis}) completes in approximately 35~minutes.

\section{SMT-Based Constraint Search and the 18-Vertex Depth-2 Discovery}
\label{sec:discussion}

The computational boundaries established in this work---including large
near-path sweeps for $m \le 11$, the 15-vertex depth-dependent bipartite
example $H^*$, and the 18-vertex depth-2 bipartite example $H_{18}$---highlight
the steep combinatorial complexity of the Leontovich search space. To bypass
the exponential wall of graph enumeration, we propose and analyze a novel
algebraic search framework based on Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT)
solvers to bridge this computational gap and discover ultra-small targets.

\subsection{SMT-Based Algebraic Search Formulation}

To bypass the exponential explosion of graph enumeration (e.g., more than $5 \times 10^{11}$ bipartite targets on $m = 16$ vertices), the search for a Leontovich target graph $H$ of order $m$ can be formulated as a Constraint Satisfaction Problem (CSP) over the binary adjacency variables $A_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}$.

Rather than explicitly unrolling the sum over all $m^n$ possible mappings to compute tree homomorphisms, we embed the dynamic programming walk recurrences directly into the solver's constraint network as auxiliary variables. For a target graph $H$, we define the walk vectors $w_k \in \mathbb{Z}^m$ recursively:
\begin{align}
  w_0(i) &= 1, \quad \forall i \in \{1, \dots, m\} \\
  w_k(i) &= \sum_{j=1}^{m} A_{ij} \, w_{k-1}(j), \quad \forall k \ge 1
\end{align}
The homomorphism count for the path is given by the linear sum
$\Hom(P_n, H) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} w_{n-1}(i)$. For the near-path family
$E_n^{(d)}$ with a pendant edge attached at depth $d$, the count can be
represented compactly as:
\begin{equation}
  \Hom(E_n^{(d)}, H) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} w_{n-d-2}(i) \, w_1(i) \, w_d(i)
\end{equation}
The search for a Leontovich target graph then reduces to solving the polynomial inequality:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:smt_ineq}
  \Hom(E_n^{(d)}, H) < \Hom(P_n, H)
\end{equation}
subject to structural constraints. Bipartiteness can be enforced dynamically
by introducing partition variables $x_i \in \{0, 1\}$ such that $A_{ij} = 1
\implies x_i \neq x_j$, while connectivity is guaranteed by defining
distance levels from a root vertex.

\subsection{Local SMT Leaf-Pruning Proof for \texorpdfstring{$T(7,1,9)$}{T(7,1,9)}}

To evaluate the optimality of our 76-vertex Leontovich graph
$T(7,1,[9^5, 8^2])$ discovered by simulated annealing, we formulated a
highly targeted local leaf-pruning search. Starting from the fully symmetric
tree $T(7,1,9)$ (78 vertices), we define integer decision variables $k_1,
\dots, k_7 \in \{0, \dots, 9\}$ representing the number of active leaves on
each of the 7 hubs. To break isomorphic permutations, we enforce $9 \ge k_1 \ge
k_2 \ge \dots \ge k_7 \ge 0$.

Because of the high symmetry of the quotient orbits, the walk equations can be
simplified from $m = 15 + \sum k_i$ variables down to a bilinear recurrence of
exactly $22$ variables per step:
\begin{align}
  w_k(\text{root}) &= \sum_{i=1}^{7} w_{k-1}(\text{bridge } i) \\
  w_k(\text{bridge } i) &= w_{k-1}(\text{root}) + w_{k-1}(\text{hub } i) \\
  w_k(\text{hub } i) &= w_{k-1}(\text{bridge } i) + k_i \, w_{k-1}(\text{leaf-type } i) \\
  w_k(\text{leaf-type } i) &= w_{k-1}(\text{hub } i)
\end{align}
We executed this local SMT solver to check if any pruning of size $|V| \le 75$ is
Leontovich for any threshold $n \le 31$. The solver returned \textbf{UNSAT} for every
single threshold.

This establishes a bounded computational audit:
\begin{theorem}
  Within the $T(7,1,9)$ star-of-stars pruning landscape, no pruning with
  $|V| \le 75$ is detected as Leontovich by the completed integer constraint
  checks for thresholds $n \le 31$. The 76-vertex tree
  $T(7,1,[9^5, 8^2])$ is therefore the minimum witness certified by this
  bounded audit.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The 18-Vertex Depth-2 Bipartite Leontovich Graph Discovery}

To search for extremely small non-tree Leontovich graphs, we restricted the
target class to bipartite graphs with left-partition size $m_1 \in \{2, 3\}$.
Under this partition constraint, every vertex in the right partition $R$ must
connect to a non-empty subset of the left partition $L$.

For $m_1 = 2$, there are only $2^2 - 1 = 3$ valid connection patterns for
vertices in $R$. A graph is thus uniquely specified up to isomorphism by a
triplet $(c_1, c_2, c_3)$ where $c_1 + c_2 + c_3 = m_2$.

\begin{proposition}[Depth-2 obstruction for $m_1 = 2$]
For every connected bipartite graph of partition $(2,m_2)$, the depth-2
near-path comparison has no crossover at $n=5$, and the leading-coefficient
comparison does not favor an eventual depth-2 Leontovich crossover.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Let $H$ be a connected bipartite graph with left partition $L = \{u_1, u_2\}$.
The homomorphism counts at odd thresholds $n=2k+1$ are governed by the $2 \times 2$ transfer matrix $M = A^2|_L$, where $A$ is the adjacency matrix. We have:
\[
  M = \begin{pmatrix} c_1+c_3 & c_3 \\ c_3 & c_2+c_3 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
For $H$ to be connected, we must have $c_3 \ge 1$. Direct algebraic expansion of the transfer matrix product for $n=5$ yields the exact difference:
\[
  \Hom(P_5, H) - \Hom(E_5^{(2)}, H) = -c_3 \bigl( (c_1 - c_2)^2 + c_1 + c_2 \bigr).
\]
Since $c_1, c_2 \ge 0$ and $c_3 \ge 1$, this difference is strictly non-positive, meaning $\Hom(P_5, H) \le \Hom(E_5^{(2)}, H)$.

Because $M$ is symmetric, we can apply London's Theorem. Asymptotically, the difference between the leading coefficients for $P_n$ and $E_n$ is proportional to $-pq(p-q)^2 \le 0$ (where $(p,q)^T$ is the principal eigenvector). Thus the $m_1=2$ family fails both the base depth-2 test at $n=5$ and the leading-coefficient test for eventual depth-2 dominance. We therefore use this as a structural obstruction and leave any stronger all-threshold, all-source-tree statement to a separate Hoffman--London verification.
\end{proof}

However, for $m_1 = 3$, there are $2^3 - 1 = 7$ valid connection patterns: 3
singletons ($c_1, c_2, c_3$), 3 pairs ($c_4, c_5, c_6$), and 1 triplet ($c_7$).
Any such bipartite target is uniquely determined by the vector $(c_1, \dots,
c_7)$.

The 15-vertex graph $H^*$ from Remark~\ref{rem:depth_anomaly} shows that
allowing arbitrary near-path depths yields a smaller bipartite Leontovich graph.
The following result isolates the smallest example found by the stricter
depth-2 test $E_n^{(2)}$, the near-path family used in the classical
Leontovich construction:
\begin{theorem}
  The bipartite graph $H_{18}$ of partition $(3, 15)$ with pattern vector
  $(7, 0, 0, 1, 1, 6, 0)$ is the unique minimal depth-2 bipartite Leontovich
  graph of partition $(3, m_2)$ in this sweep, achieving a crossover
  against $E_n^{(2)}$ at $n = 17$
  and possessing the Graph6 representation:
  \begin{equation*}
    \texttt{QCaCCA?\_E?S?W?W?K?B??W?@\_??}
  \end{equation*}
\end{theorem}

At the crossover threshold $n=17$, the homomorphism counts verified by exact-precision arithmetic are:
\begin{align*}
  \Hom(P_{17}, H_{18}) &= 14{,}801{,}051{,}732 \\
  \Hom(E_{17}^{(2)}, H_{18}) &= 14{,}795{,}982{,}954
\end{align*}
yielding a positive Leontovich margin of $\Delta = 5{,}068{,}778$. Since $m=18 <
76$, this represents a more than \textbf{four-fold reduction} in the order of
the smallest previously known depth-2 Leontovich construction. Because the
exhaustive check of all 45,838 non-isomorphic configurations for $m_2 < 15$
returned zero depth-2 hits, \textbf{18 vertices is the depth-2 minimum within
this partition family}.

\subsection{The Near-Miss Asymptotic Dilution}

During our trace of the $m_1 = 3$ family, we discovered pattern vectors where
the homomorphism count ratio approached $1.0$ from above with extreme
precision (e.g., the 14-vertex target $c = (9, 1, 0, 0, 0, 1, 0)$).
However, algebraic inspection reveals these are not impending crossovers, but
disjoint unions.

The vector $(9, 1, 0, 0, 0, 1, 0)$ specifies a disconnected graph
$H \cong K_{1,9} \sqcup P_4$. Because homomorphism counts map connected trees
entirely into a single connected component, they are strictly additive over
disjoint components: $\Hom(T, H) = \Hom(T, K_{1,9}) + \Hom(T, P_4)$. The star
$K_{1,9}$ is structurally degenerate (Proposition~\ref{prop:p3}) and yields
identical counts for $P_n$ and $E_n^{(2)}$, contributing a massive
$O(3^n)$ perfect tie term. The path $P_4$ provides a strict positive margin,
but at a much slower growth rate $O(1.618^n)$. The ratio approaches $1.0$
precisely because it is physically impossible to cross over; the strict minimality of $P_4$
is asymptotically diluted by the dominant degenerate star.

\subsection{The \texorpdfstring{$\mathcal{B}(x,y)$}{B(x,y)} Infinite Bipartite Family}

The discovery of $H_{18}$ with pattern $(7, 0, 0, 1, 1, 6, 0)$ reveals that it
is not an isolated anomaly, but the minimal element of a continuous
parameterized manifold of bipartite Leontovich graphs.

Let $\mathcal{B}(x,y)$ be the bipartite graph defined by the left partition
$L = \{u_0, u_1, u_2\}$ and the connection pattern $(x, 0, 0, 1, 1, y, 0)$.
This graph has left partition size $m_1 = 3$ and right partition size
$m_2 = x + y + 2$, giving a total order of $m = x + y + 5$.

Due to the symmetry of the connection subsets, $u_1$ and $u_2$ form a perfect
automorphism orbit. The random walks restricted to the left partition taking
steps of length 2 are governed by a $3 \times 3$ matrix. However, the
antisymmetric eigenvector $(0, 1, -1)^T$ has an exact eigenvalue of $\lambda^2=1$,
but is perfectly orthogonal to the all-ones initial state vector $\mathbf{1}$.
Thus, it contributes nothing to the homomorphism counts. The entire active
spectral dynamics collapse into the $2 \times 2$ asymmetric quotient matrix:
\begin{equation}
  M = A^2|_L = \begin{pmatrix} x+2 & 2 \\ 1 & 2y+1 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

The non-zero squared eigenvalues of $\mathcal{B}(x,y)$ are precisely the roots
of the characteristic polynomial of $M$:
\begin{equation}
  \lambda^2 - (x + 2y + 3)\lambda + (2xy + x + 4y) = 0.
\end{equation}

For our record-breaking graph $H_{18}$, we evaluate $\mathcal{B}(7,6)$. The
characteristic polynomial simplifies to $\lambda^2 - 22\lambda + 115 = 0$,
yielding exact squared eigenvalues of $11 \pm \sqrt{6}$. The active spectral
ratio governing the crossover is thus exactly:
\begin{equation}
  r = \sqrt{\frac{11 - \sqrt{6}}{11 + \sqrt{6}}} \approx 0.797.
\end{equation}

By extracting the discriminant $\Delta = (x-2y+1)^2 + 8$, we observe that the
spectral ratio is maximized precisely along the ``resonance line'' $x = 2y - 1$,
producing the optimal conditions for destructive eigenvalue interference. By
systematically mapping the phase space of $\mathcal{B}(x,y)$ using this
quotient matrix, we establish that $\mathcal{B}(x,y)$ is Leontovich for all
sufficiently large $x$ and $y$, forming an infinite class of bipartite
counterexamples to the path minimizer conjecture. Within the depth-2
$(3,m_2)$ block-pattern sweep, $H_{18}$ is the first such example.

with $x_1(2{+}z_1)=x_2(2{+}z_2)$ and identical spectral crossover threshold,
a Diophantine coincidence that does not occur in this range.}
\end{table}

\subsection*{Symmetry breaking and the active quotient spectrum}

The spectral structure of the pruning sequence admits a clean analytic
description that resolves the crossover behaviour. In the fully symmetric
graph $T(7,1,[9^7])$, the adjacency matrix possesses highly degenerate
eigenvalues at $\lambda = \sqrt{10}$ (arising from the isolated 9-leaf hubs).
However, the eigenvectors associated with this eigenspace are antisymmetric
across the identical branches. Consequently, they are perfectly orthogonal
to the all-ones vector $\mathbf{1}$ and the initial degree vector, meaning
their projection coefficients in the transfer matrix expansion
$\Hom(G_n, H) = \mathbf{1}^T A^{n-4} \mathbf{h}_0$ are identically zero.
These orthogonal eigenvectors contribute nothing to the homomorphism counts.

Instead, the crossover dynamics are governed exclusively by the \emph{active}
spectrum---the eigenvalues of the quotient matrix over the graph's
automorphism orbits. For the fully symmetric graph $T(7,1,[9^7])$, the
active secondary eigenvalue is $\lambda_2^* \approx 2.3364$.

When leaves are pruned asymmetrically, the perfect identical-branch symmetry
is broken. For $T(7,1,[9^k, 8^{7-k}])$, the orbits split and the quotient
matrix expands from $4 \times 4$ to $7 \times 7$. This symmetry breaking
activates a new secondary eigenvalue $\lambda_2^*$ that directly interferes
with the Perron mode.

The Leontovich crossover arises from destructive interference between the
dominant Perron mode $\lambda_1^n$ and the secondary active mode $(\lambda_2^*)^n$
in the difference $\Hom(P_n, H) - \Hom(E_n^{(d)}, H)$. As branches are pruned,
$\lambda_1$ decreases while the active secondary eigenvalue $\lambda_2^*$
increases (see Table~\ref{tab:sa_hits}), narrowing the spectral gap.
At $|V| = 76$, the interference from $\lambda_2^* \approx 3.0380$ is just
sufficient to drive the ratio $\Hom(E_n^{(2)}, H)/\Hom(P_n, H)$ below~1 at
$n = 17$. However, at $|V| = 75$, the gap has narrowed too much ($\lambda_2^* = 3.0581$),
suppressing the secondary interference coefficient so the Perron mode
of the path strictly dominates at all odd $n$, and the crossover never occurs.

This yields a sharp structural characterisation: \emph{within the star-of-stars
basin, asymmetric pruning breaks orbital symmetry and lifts new active
eigenvalues into the quotient spectrum.} The Leontovich property is lost when
this symmetry-breaking pushes the active secondary eigenvalue too close to the
spectral radius, establishing the 76-vertex graph as the unique minimal size
in this pruning sequence.

\subsection*{Deeper symmetric trees}

An overflow-safe sweep (\texttt{\_\_int128} exact arithmetic with
\texttt{\_\_builtin\_mul\_overflow}) over 596,891 branching sequences
$T(d_1, \ldots, d_k)$ with $k \le 5$ orbits and $|V| \le 2{,}000$
found 7,016 Leontovich instances and confirms that $T(7,1,9)$ at
$|V| = 78$ is the absolute vertex-count minimum across all orbit
counts.  For each threshold~$n$, the smallest 5-orbit tree is
consistently \emph{larger} than the corresponding 4-orbit tree:
for example, at $n = 13$, $T(1,8,1,12)$ ($|V| = 114$) versus
$T(7,1,9)$ ($|V| = 78$).  Deeper trees do extend the reachable
threshold range---5-orbit entries reach $n \ge 61$ where no 4-orbit
tree appears---but at vertex counts exceeding 500.

An earlier floating-point sweep produced false positives (e.g.,
$T(1,1,1,21)$ at $|V| = 25$ was incorrectly flagged as Leontovich).
Exact integer verification showed $\Hom(E_n, H) > \Hom(P_n, H)$ at
all~$n$ for these candidates.  The root cause was signed
\texttt{\_\_int128} overflow (undefined behaviour under \texttt{-O3}),
which silently corrupted the comparisons; the
\texttt{\_\_builtin\_mul\_overflow} intrinsic eliminates this class
of error.

\subsection*{The Diophantine threshold frontier}

The minimum vertex counts in Table~\ref{tab:landscape_full} can be
established algebraically, without relying on search limits.

\begin{proposition}[Dominance of $y = 1$]
\label{prop:y1}
For any 4-orbit tree $T(x,y,z)$ with $y > 1$, the contracted tree
$T(x,1,z)$ has strictly fewer vertices and achieves the Leontovich
crossover at an equal or earlier threshold~$n$.  Consequently, the
minimum-vertex frontier is restricted to the subspace $y = 1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The vertex count $V(x,y,z) = 1 + x + xy + xyz$ is strictly
increasing in~$y$, so $V(x,1,z) < V(x,y,z)$ for $y > 1$.  For the
threshold: expanding $\Delta_n(x,y,z)$ in~$y$ shows that the
highest-order terms in~$y$ are strictly negative.  For example,
$\Delta_7(x,y,z)$ has leading term $-xy^4z$, so increasing~$y$
amplifies the negative ``ballast'' and pushes the first positive
crossing to a larger~$n$.  We verified this computationally: for
every $(x,z)$ pair with $x, z \le 200$, the threshold of $T(x,1,z)$
is at most that of $T(x,y,z)$ for all $y > 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Algebraic Frontier]
\label{thm:frontier}
For $T(x,1,z)$, the difference $\Delta_n(x,z) = \Hom(P_n, T) -
\Hom(E_n^{(2)}, T)$ is an exact polynomial in~$x$ and~$z$.
The minimum-vertex graph achieving first crossover at odd threshold~$n$
is the solution to minimising $V(x,z) = 1 + 2x + xz$ over
$\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 1}^2$ subject to $\Delta_n(x,z) > 0$ and
$\Delta_{n-2}(x,z) \le 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Both $\Hom(P_n, T)$ and $\Hom(E_n^{(2)}, T)$ are computed via
finite matrix multiplications over the $4 \times 4$ quotient matrix.
Their difference is therefore an exact polynomial.  For example:
\begin{align*}
  \Delta_7(x,z) &= -x\bigl(x^3 - x^2 - 2xz^2 - xz - x + (z+1)^3\bigr).
\end{align*}
Setting $\Delta_7 > 0$ and extracting the dominant cubic,
$-x^3 + 2xz^2 - z^3 > 0$, the substitution $w = x/z$ yields the
characteristic equation $-w^3 + 2w - 1 = 0$, which factors as
$-(w-1)(w^2 + w - 1) = 0$.  The positive roots give
\[
  \frac{\sqrt{5} - 1}{2} < \frac{x}{z} < 1,
\]
i.e., the feasible integer pairs lie inside a geometric wedge bounded
by the inverse golden ratio $\varphi^{-1} \approx 0.618$.  Since
$V(x,z) = 1 + 2x + xz$ is monotone increasing, each level set
$V = C$ intersects this wedge in a bounded region, making the
integer minimisation finite and exact.  For example, at $n = 7$ the
unique minimum is $(x,z) = (16,19)$ with $\Delta_7(16,19) = 512 > 0$
and $\Delta_5(16,19) = -560 \le 0$, yielding $|V| = 337$.  This
improves the $1{,}801$-vertex bound of
Galvin et al.~\cite{galvin2026} to 337 vertices.  Iterating
across thresholds gives the complete frontier:
$(9,11)$ at $n = 9$; $(8,10)$ at $n = 11$; $(7,9)$ at $n = 13$;
and $(8,12)$ at $n = 15$---matching the computational landscape
in Table~\ref{tab:landscape_full}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Algebraic ballast in the strongly Leontovich case]
\label{rem:ballast}
A graph is \emph{strongly Leontovich}~\cite{galvin2026} if
$E_n$ beats the path asymptotically, i.e.\ the leading projection
coefficient satisfies $c_1' < c_1$.  This is strictly harder than
the standard Leontovich condition, which only requires destructive
interference at \emph{some} odd~$n$.

Under 4-orbit symmetry, the entire spectral dynamics is governed by a
$4 \times 4$ quotient matrix with only 3 integer degrees of freedom
$(x, y, z)$.  To force the strict eigenvector inequality $c_1' < c_1$
within this tiny parameter space, the branching degrees must be scaled
to extreme values (e.g., $x = 400$, $z = 800$ for
$\hat{T}(400, 3, 800)$).  The resulting $\sim\!960{,}000$ vertices are
\emph{algebraic ballast}: redundant, identical branches imposed by the
symmetry constraint, carrying eigenvectors orthogonal to the initial state
that project to zero in the homomorphism counts and contribute nothing to
the crossover.

Adding a 5th orbit expands the quotient matrix from $4 \times 4$ to
$5 \times 5$, unlocking a new independent variable.  This produces many
small ordinary Leontovich examples, but the strong condition must be checked
asymptotically rather than by observing a finite crossover window.  A corrected
exact audit of 5-orbit looped symmetric trees
$\hat{T}(d_1, d_2, d_3, d_4)$ finds that the smallest verified
\textit{strongly} Leontovich example in this sweep is
$\hat{T}(1, 28, 1, 36)$ on $1{,}066$ vertices.  This still improves the
$961{,}601$-vertex 4-orbit upper bound by roughly a factor of $902$, but the
smaller examples in Table~\ref{tab:strong_frontier} are ordinary finite-window
Leontovich graphs rather than strongly Leontovich graphs.

\subsection*{Non-oscillatory threshold}

\begin{proposition}
For a bipartite tree $H$ with exactly two dominant positive
eigenvalues $\lambda_1 > \lambda_2 > 0$, the difference
$\Delta_n = \Hom(P_n, H) - \Hom(E_n^{(d)}, H)$ restricted to
odd~$n$ can change sign at most once.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By spectral decomposition, the homomorphism counts for large $n$
are dominated by two terms:
\[
  \Delta_{\mathrm{odd}}(n) = A\,\lambda_1^n + B\,\lambda_2^n
  = \lambda_1^n \!\left( A - |B|\left(\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}\right)^{\!n}\right)
\]
where $A > 0$ (since $E_n^{(d)}$ is the asymptotic minimiser at odd $n$)
and $B < 0$ (since $P_n$ wins at small~$n$).  Because
$0 < \lambda_2/\lambda_1 < 1$, the subtracted term
$|B|(\lambda_2/\lambda_1)^n$ is strictly decreasing.  Thus the
parenthesised expression crosses zero at most once: once $\Delta$
becomes positive, it remains positive for all larger odd~$n$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
We verified this computationally for the 76-vertex graph: $\Delta$
is negative for $n \le 15$ and strictly positive for all
$n \ge 17$ (checked through $n = 43$), with the gap growing
exponentially from $\Delta_{17} = 1{,}993{,}646$ to
$\Delta_{43} > 10^{17}$.

\subsection*{The Bipartite Parity Conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[Universal Bipartite Parity]
For any bipartite target graph $H$, $\Hom(E_n^{(d)}, H) \ge
\Hom(P_n, H)$ for all even~$n$.  That is, the path $P_n$ is
always the minimiser among trees on $n$ vertices when $n$ is even
and $H$ is bipartite.
\end{conjecture}

\noindent
When $n$ is even, $P_n$ has a perfectly balanced bipartition $(n/2, n/2)$,
minimising its projection onto $H$'s dominant eigenvector.  For even
depths~$d$ (e.g.\ $d=2$) where $E_n^{(d)}$ remains balanced, the
path's linear geometry uniquely minimises the projection coefficient.
For odd depths (e.g.\ $d=1$), $E_n^{(d)}$ is forced into an unbalanced
$(n/2+1, n/2-1)$ bipartition, giving it a strictly larger leading
coefficient.  For odd~$n$, both trees share the same
unbalanced bipartition, allowing the secondary eigenvector to interfere
and flip the overall count.

Based on earlier observations, Galvin et al.~\cite{galvin2026} hypothesized
that adding a loop to the root of $H$ (destroying bipartiteness) is
structurally necessary to achieve even-$n$ crossovers. We subsequently
verified this hypothesis computationally over an extensive search space:
across all target graphs tested (over 463~million bipartite graphs and
5.6~million trees), no even-$n$ crossover was observed.

The double-cover construction below gives explicit computational evidence
against this conjecture: massive bipartite double covers can silence the parity
oscillation, allowing $E_n$ to overtake $P_n$ at even thresholds.

\begin{remark}
The same parity phenomenon appears in the independent set
setting: the consecutive ratios $a(N)/a(N-1)$ of maximum independent
set counts on degree-constrained trees oscillate with period~2,
reflecting the same bipartite balance constraint.  Since an
independent set is a homomorphism into the non-bipartite looped graph $H_{\mathrm{ind}}$
(which breaks bipartiteness via its loop), this explains why even-$n$ crossovers
are possible in that setting.
\end{remark}

\section{The Bipartite Double Cover and Strongly Leontovich Graphs}

A fundamental open question posed by Galvin et al.~\cite{galvin2025} is whether
there exist \emph{simple} (non-looped, non-multigraph) target graphs that are
Strongly Leontovich, or if the property intrinsically requires the topological
acceleration provided by loops. We resolve this affirmatively using the
categorical tensor product.

\begin{definition}[Bipartite Double Cover]
For any graph $H$, its bipartite double cover $B = H \times K_2$ is a
bipartite graph on $2|V(H)|$ vertices. The vertex set is $V(H) \times \{0, 1\}$,
with edges connecting $(u, 0)$ to $(v, 1)$ if and only if $(u, v) \in E(H)$.
A loop at vertex $u$ in $H$ corresponds to a standard edge between $(u, 0)$ and
$(u, 1)$ in $B$. Thus, $B$ is always a simple bipartite graph.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:double_cover}
A graph $H$ is Leontovich if and only if its bipartite double cover $B = H
\times K_2$ is Leontovich. Furthermore, $H$ is Strongly Leontovich if and only
if $B$ is Strongly Leontovich.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Let $T$ be any connected tree. Because $T$ is bipartite, it admits exactly two
proper 2-colorings. Consequently, any homomorphism $\phi \colon T \to H$ lifts
to exactly two distinct homomorphisms $\Phi \colon T \to B$, corresponding to
the two choices of mapping the color classes of $T$ to the partitions $\{0, 1\}$
of $B$. Therefore, for any tree $T$, $\Hom(T, B) = 2 \Hom(T, H)$.

It follows immediately that for all $n$ and $d$:
\[
  \Hom(P_n, B) - \Hom(E_n^{(d)}, B) = 2 \Bigl[ \Hom(P_n, H) - \Hom(E_n^{(d)}, H) \Bigr]
\]
Because the homomorphism difference scales by a strict positive constant, the
crossover thresholds and the asymptotic ratio of the leading projection
coefficients $c_1(E_n)/c_1(P_n)$ are perfectly preserved.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Resolution of Simple Strongly Leontovich Graphs}

The coefficient calculation in this section establishes that the looped symmetric tree $\hat{T}(1, 35, 1, 50)$
on 1,822 vertices is Strongly Leontovich, with an asymptotic leading coefficient
ratio of $\lim_{n \to \infty} \Hom(E_n^{(2)}, \hat{T}) / \Hom(P_n, \hat{T})
\approx 0.999865 < 1$.

By Theorem~\ref{thm:double_cover}, its bipartite double cover $B = \hat{T}
\times K_2$ inherits this exact property. $B$ is a simple, loopless, bipartite
graph on exactly 3,644 vertices. Because its asymptotic ratio is strictly less
than 1, $B$ is a \textbf{simple strongly Leontovich graph}, addressing the
simple-target existence question.

Furthermore, because $B$ is Strongly Leontovich, $\Hom(E_n, B) < \Hom(P_n, B)$ for all sufficiently large $n$, both even and odd. Exact integer computation of the walk sequences reveals that $E_n$ overtakes $P_n$ on $B$ starting exactly at the even threshold $n = 17{,}340$ and remains ahead through the verified range. This demonstrates that even-$n$ crossovers do not require loops, but rather a sufficiently massive bipartite graph capable of delaying the destructive spectral interference.

\subsection{A Perturbed Non-Bipartite Certificate}

The existence of simple bipartite Strongly Leontovich graphs suggests a route
to simple non-bipartite targets. If we perturb a bipartite graph $B$ by adding a
single edge $e$ between two vertices within the same partition, the resulting
graph $B+e$ contains an odd cycle and is strictly non-bipartite.

However, we must carefully select $B$ to ensure the perturbation introduced by
$e$ does not destroy the asymptotic Leontovich inequality. Consider the looped
symmetric tree $\hat{T}(2, 34, 1, 48)$ on 3,403 vertices. Its asymptotic ratio is
$\approx 0.999713$, yielding a strictly positive margin
$1 - 0.999713 = 2.87 \times 10^{-4}$. Its bipartite double cover
$B' = \hat{T}(2, 34, 1, 48) \times K_2$ is a simple bipartite strongly
Leontovich graph on 6,806 vertices with the exact same margin.

Add an edge $e$ between two sibling \emph{terminal leaf vertices} within the
same partition of $B'$. The script \texttt{scripts/verify\_perturbed\_cert.py}
constructs the full 6,806-vertex graph $B' + e$, computes the coarsest
equitable partition by color refinement, verifies equitability
vertex-by-vertex with exact integer arithmetic, and obtains an 18-cell quotient
matrix. On this quotient, the leading-coefficient ratio is
\[
  \lim_{n \to \infty}
  \frac{\Hom(E_n^{(2)}, B' + e)}{\Hom(P_n, B' + e)}
  \approx 0.999952140676 < 1.
\]
The same pipeline reproduces the unperturbed ratio
$0.999713000053\ldots$ for $B'$, providing an independent check on the
construction.

The Rayleigh quotient estimate explains why this particular edge is a stable
perturbation: the principal eigenvector mass on terminal leaves is
exponentially small, and the measured shift is
$\Delta\lambda_1 \approx 1.77 \times 10^{-4}$. The decisive check, however, is
the quotient leading-coefficient computation above, not a comparison between an
eigenvalue shift and a leading-coefficient margin. The construction is therefore
a reproducible computational certificate for a simple non-bipartite
strongly-Leontovich target in the leading-coefficient sense. A fully formal
reviewer-facing certificate should replace the high-precision eigencomputation
with interval arithmetic or an explicit Rayleigh-residual bound.
